SYSTEMIC INEQUALITY

Racism is not only about individual actions, it is embedded in systems that shape access to education, housing, employment, healthcare, and justice.

WHAT MAKES IT SYSTEMIC?

Patterns

It appears across communities, institutions, and generations.

Policies

It is reinforced through laws, practices, and institutional decisions.

Access

It shapes who receives resources, protection, and opportunity.

Impact

It affects everyday life even when it is not immediately visible.

SYSTEM AREAS

HOUSING

Where you live shapes access to opportunity. Historic practices like redlining and discriminatory lending created lasting divides in neighborhoods, wealth, and resources.

Housing policies have shaped communities and opportunity for generations.

EDUCATION

Schools are often funded by local property taxes, meaning communities with fewer resources may receive less funding and fewer opportunities.

Educational access can differ depending on location and resources.

CRIMINAL JUSTICE

Disparities in policing, sentencing, and incarceration rates reflect deeper systemic patterns shaped by policies and enforcement practices over time.

Justice systems can produce unequal outcomes.

EMPLOYMENT & WEALTH

Access to jobs, fair wages, and generational wealth is influenced by systemic barriers in hiring, pay, housing, and education.

Economic opportunity is often shaped beyond individual control.

HEALTHCARE

Access to healthcare and health outcomes are influenced by where people live, work, and the resources available to them.

Health outcomes are shaped by access, resources, and systemic conditions.

THE BIG IDEA

Systemic inequality is powerful because it can operate quietly. It is not always obvious, but its effects can be seen in outcomes across society.

WHY SYSTEMIC INEQUALITY MATTERS

Systemic inequality operates in ways that are not always visible, but deeply affect everyday life. Recognizing these patterns is a step toward understanding and addressing the root causes of inequality.
