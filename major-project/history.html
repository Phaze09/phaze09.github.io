HISTORY OF RACIAL INEQUALITY IN AMERICA

Racial inequality in America did not happen by chance, it was built over time through laws, systems, and decisions. Understanding this history helps explain why inequality still exists today.

SLAVERY (1619–1865)

Inequality in America began with the forced labor and dehumanization of enslaved Africans. Entire economic systems were built on exploitation, establishing a racial hierarchy that would shape the country for centuries.

Enslaved people working on plantations, where labor built wealth that they were denied.

RECONSTRUCTION & BACKLASH (1865–1900s)

After slavery ended, there was a brief period of progress as Black Americans gained rights and political representation. But this progress was quickly met with resistance, as new laws and systems were created to limit freedom and maintain control.

Image Placeholder

Black lawmakers during Reconstruction, a moment of progress that was quickly undermined.

JIM CROW ERA (Late 1800s–1960s)

Segregation became law, enforcing separation in schools, housing, and public spaces. Inequality was no longer hidden—it was written into everyday life, backed by violence and intimidation.

Image Placeholder

“Separate but equal” policies that enforced inequality across the country.

CIVIL RIGHTS MOVEMENT (1950s–1960s)

People across the country organized, protested, and demanded change. Photography and media played a critical role, exposing injustice to the world and forcing the nation to confront it.

Image Placeholder

Images from the Civil Rights Movement brought national attention to injustice and resistance.

MODERN SYSTEMS OF INEQUALITY

Although laws changed, inequality did not disappear, it evolved. Practices like redlining, disparities in education, and mass incarceration continue to shape opportunity and outcomes today.

Image Placeholder

Historic housing policies continue to shape neighborhoods and opportunity today.

WHY THIS HISTORY MATTERS

The systems that exist today are rooted in the past. By understanding how inequality was created, we can better recognize it in the present and work toward meaningful change.
